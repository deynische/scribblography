Minimizing Filler Words

28 Nov 2018 ｜ 2 min read
writing

I have a tendency to use a lot of filler words. They are so heavily embedded in my speech patterns that they weave themselves effortlessly into my writing. I’ve been actively trying to edit them out for a little over a year.

Maybe I just like filler words too much. I find comfort in being noncommittal especially as I continue to discover the limits to my knowledge. I never want to promise something only to find out I’m horribly wrong. However, the other side of all this is an image that I’m insecure and doubtful. In my effort to be too honest, I’m inadvertently telling you not to have any faith in me.

This is the problem with filler words.

Filler words pad and minimize content. The words just and only conjures up the idea of toeing a threshold. Approximations like almost and probably reveal doubt. Some words can be very redundant and really don’t add much to a sentence.

These words have a time and place. In fact, they’re quite useful if you’re trying to assure an issue isn’t really that complicated. The solution you have will probably work. It’s just going to take a little extra time.

See how soft and beguiling those sentences have become? It almost sounds like customer service speak, a style intended for emotional situations. For example: the retentions department of your local telecom company.

Remember, filler words are sneaky. Understand them. Recognize them. Learn when to use them.
